Types of Forests

Tropical Rainforests

Tropical rainforests are rainforests that occur in areas of tropical rainforest climate in which there is no dry season all months have an average precipitation of at least 60 mm (2.4 in) and may also be referred to as lowland equatorial evergreen rainforest. True rainforests are typically found between 10 degrees north and south of the equator, and are a sub-set of the tropical forest biome that occurs roughly within the 28-degree latitudes (in the equatorial zone between the Tropic of Cancer and Tropic of Capricorn).

  • Climate

    Tropical rainforests are characterized by a warm and wet climate with no substantial dry season. Mean monthly temperatures exceed 18 °C (64 °F) during all months of the year. Average annual rainfall is no less than 168 cm (66 in) and can exceed 1,000 cm (390 in) although it typically lies between 175 cm (69 in) and 200 cm (79 in).

  • Vegetation

    Tropical rainforests are home to an incredibly diverse range of plant life, with over half of the world's known plant species found in these forests. The vegetation is characterized by tall, evergreen trees with broad leaves. Lianas (climbing plants) and epiphytes (plants that grow on other plants) are also common.

  • Fauna

    Tropical rainforests are also home to a wide variety of animal life, including insects, birds, mammals, reptiles, and amphibians. Some of the most iconic rainforest animals include monkeys, snakes, jaguars, toucans, and parrots.

  • Biodiversity

    Tropical rainforests are the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth. They are home to over half of the world's known species of plants and animals. This biodiversity is due to the warm and wet climate, which provides ideal conditions for life to thrive.

  • Importance

    Tropical rainforests play an important role in the global environment. They help to regulate the Earth's climate, provide clean water and air, and are home to a wide variety of medicinal plants. Rainforests are also important cultural and spiritual sites for many indigenous peoples.

  • Threats

    Tropical rainforests are facing a number of threats, including deforestation, logging, mining, and climate change. Deforestation is the clearing of rainforests for other uses, such as agriculture and development. This is a major problem because it destroys habitat and reduces biodiversity. Logging and mining also damage rainforests and contribute to deforestation. Climate change is also a threat to rainforests because it is causing more extreme weather events, such as droughts and floods.

  • Conservation

    There are a number of efforts underway to conserve tropical rainforests. These efforts include protected areas, sustainable forestry practices, and education campaigns. It is important to protect tropical rainforests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

Temperate Forests

Temperate forests are forests that occur in areas with temperate climates. Temperate climates are characterized by warm summers and cool winters. Temperate forests are found in the mid-latitudes of the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

  • Climate

    Temperate forests have a warm summer climate with average temperatures ranging from 10 to 25 degrees Celsius (50 to 77 degrees Fahrenheit). Winters are cool with average temperatures ranging from -5 to 10 degrees Celsius (23 to 50 degrees Fahrenheit). Temperate forests receive an average of 50 to 200 centimeters (20 to 79 inches) of precipitation per year.

  • Vegetation

    Temperate forests are home to a variety of plant life, including trees, shrubs, and wildflowers. Some of the most common trees found in temperate forests include oak, maple, birch, and pine. Shrubs such as rhododendron, blueberry, and blackberry are also common. Wildflowers such as wildflowers, azaleas, and daffodils bloom in the spring and summer.

  • Fauna

    Temperate forests are also home to a variety of animals, including mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, and insects. Some of the most common mammals found in temperate forests include deer, bears, foxes, and rabbits. Birds such as owls, hawks, woodpeckers, and songbirds are also common. Reptiles such as snakes and lizards are also found in temperate forests. Amphibians such as frogs and salamanders are also common. Insects such as butterflies, beetles, and ants are also found in temperate forests.

  • Biodiversity

    Temperate forests are home to a variety of plant and animal life. However, the biodiversity of temperate forests is not as high as that of tropical rainforests. This is because temperate forests have a shorter growing season and less sunlight than tropical rainforests.

  • Importance

    Temperate forests play an important role in the global environment. They help to regulate the Earth's climate, provide clean water and air, and are home to a variety of medicinal plants. Temperate forests are also important cultural and spiritual sites for many indigenous peoples.

  • Threats

    Temperate forests are facing a number of threats, including deforestation, pollution, and climate change. Deforestation is the clearing of forests for other uses, such as agriculture and development. This is a major problem because it destroys habitat and reduces biodiversity. Pollution from factories and vehicles can also damage temperate forests. Climate change is also a threat to temperate forests because it is causing more extreme weather events, such as droughts and floods.

  • Conservation

    There are a number of efforts underway to conserve temperate forests. These efforts include protected areas, sustainable forestry practices, and education campaigns. It is important to protect temperate forests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

Boreal Forests(TAIGA)

Boreal forests, also known as taiga, are forests that occur in the northernmost parts of the Northern Hemisphere. They are characterized by cold winters and short summers. Boreal forests are found in Canada, Russia, Scandinavia, and Alaska.

  • Climate

    Boreal forests have a cold climate with average temperatures ranging from -5 to 10 degrees Celsius (23 to 50 degrees Fahrenheit) in winter and 10 to 25 degrees Celsius (50 to 77 degrees Fahrenheit) in summer. Winters are long and dark, with little sunlight. Summers are short and warm, with long days and nights. Boreal forests receive an average of 25 to 50 centimeters (10 to 20 inches) of precipitation per year.

  • Vegetation

    Boreal forests are home to a variety of coniferous trees, including spruce, fir, pine, and larch. These trees are adapted to the cold climate and have needles that help to reduce water loss. Boreal forests also have a ground layer of mosses, lichens, and wildflowers.

  • Fauna

    Boreal forests are home to a variety of animals, including mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, and insects. Some of the most common mammals found in boreal forests include moose, deer, wolves, bears, and foxes. Birds such as owls, hawks, woodpeckers, and songbirds are also common. Reptiles such as snakes and lizards are also found in boreal forests. Amphibians such as frogs and salamanders are also common. Insects such as mosquitoes, blackflies, and beetles are also found in boreal forests.

  • Biodiversity

    Boreal forests have a lower biodiversity than tropical rainforests or temperate forests. This is because the cold climate and short growing season limit the number of species that can survive. However, boreal forests are still home to a variety of unique plants and animals.

  • Importance

    Boreal forests play an important role in the global environment. They help to regulate the Earth's climate, provide clean water and air, and are home to a variety of medicinal plants. Boreal forests are also important cultural and spiritual sites for many indigenous peoples.

  • Threats

    Boreal forests are facing a number of threats, including climate change, logging, and mining. Climate change is causing the boreal forest to warm, which is leading to changes in vegetation and wildlife. Logging and mining also damage boreal forests and contribute to deforestation.

  • Conservation

    There are a number of efforts underway to conserve boreal forests. These efforts include protected areas, sustainable forestry practices, and education campaigns. It is important to protect boreal forests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

Mangrove Forests

Mangrove forests are forests that grow in coastal areas in tropical and subtropical regions. They are characterized by trees and shrubs that are adapted to living in salty water and tidal mudflats. Mangrove forests are found in over 100 countries around the world.

  • Climate

    Mangrove forests grow in warm, humid climates with high rainfall. The average temperature ranges from 25 to 32 degrees Celsius (77 to 90 degrees Fahrenheit). Mangrove forests receive an average of 1,500 to 4,500 millimeters (60 to 180 inches) of rainfall per year.

  • Vegetation

    Mangrove forests are home to a variety of trees and shrubs, including red mangrove, black mangrove, white mangrove, and buttonwood. These trees and shrubs have adapted to living in salty water and tidal mudflats. They have roots that help them to anchor themselves in the mud and breathe underwater. Mangrove forests also have a ground layer of algae, grasses, and oysters.

  • Fauna

    Mangrove forests are home to a variety of animals, including mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, and insects. Some of the most common mammals found in mangrove forests include crocodiles, monkeys, and opossums. Birds such as herons, egrets, and kingfishers are also common. Reptiles such as snakes and lizards are also found in mangrove forests. Amphibians such as frogs and salamanders are also common. Insects such as mosquitoes, crabs, and shrimp are also found in mangrove forests.

  • Biodiversity

    Mangrove forests are highly biodiverse ecosystems. They are home to a variety of plant and animal life, including many species that are found nowhere else on Earth. Mangrove forests are important nursery habitats for many fish and shellfish species. They are also a refuge for many migratory birds.

  • Importance

    Mangrove forests play an important role in the global environment. They help to protect coastlines from erosion and storms. They also filter pollutants from the water and provide habitat for a variety of marine life. Mangrove forests are also important cultural and spiritual sites for many indigenous peoples.

  • Threats

    Mangrove forests are facing a number of threats, including pollution, development, and climate change. Pollution from factories and farms can damage mangrove forests and kill marine life. Development projects such as shrimp farms and tourism can destroy mangrove forests. Climate change is causing sea levels to rise, which is inundating mangrove forests and making them more vulnerable to storms.

  • Conservation

    There are a number of efforts underway to conserve mangrove forests. These efforts include protected areas, sustainable management practices, and education campaigns. It is important to protect mangrove forests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

Montane Forests

Montane forests are forests that occur at high elevations in mountains. They are characterized by cool temperatures, high rainfall, and shorter growing seasons than lower elevation forests. Montane forests are found in all continents except Antarctica.

  • Climate

Summers are short and mild, with less rainfall. Montane forests receive an average of 1,000 to 3,000 millimeters (40 to 120 inches) of precipitation per year.

  • Vegetation

    Montane forests are home to a variety of trees, shrubs, and wildflowers. Some of the most common trees found in montane forests include fir, spruce, pine, birch, and maple. Shrubs such as rhododendron, blueberry, and blackberry are also common. Wildflowers such as wildflowers, azaleas, and daffodils bloom in the spring and summer.

  • Fauna

Reptiles such as snakes and lizards are also found in montane forests. Amphibians such as frogs and salamanders are also common. Insects such as butterflies, beetles, and ants are also found in montane forests.

  • Biodiversity

    Montane forests are often home to a high diversity of plant and animal life. This is because montane forests provide a variety of habitats, including forests, meadows, streams, and waterfalls. Montane forests are also important migration corridors for many animals.

  • Importance

    Montane forests play an important role in the global environment. They help to regulate the Earth's climate, provide clean water and air, and are home to a variety of medicinal plants. Montane forests are also important cultural and spiritual sites for many indigenous peoples.

  • Threats

Deforestation is the clearing of forests for other uses, such as agriculture and development. This is a major problem because it destroys habitat and reduces biodiversity. Climate change is causing montane forests to warm, which is leading to changes in vegetation and wildlife. Pollution from factories and vehicles can also damage montane forests.

It is important to protect montane forests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

Deciduous Forests

  • Vegetation

  • Importance

Dry(XERIC) Forests

Dry forests also known as xeric forests, are forests that occur in areas with low rainfall. They are characterized by drought-tolerant trees and shrubs, and a sparse understory. Dry forests are found all over the world, in tropical, subtropical, and temperate regions.

  • Climate

    Dry forests receive an average of 250 to 750 millimeters (10 to 30 inches) of precipitation per year. This precipitation is often concentrated in a short wet season, followed by a long dry season. Dry forests have warm temperatures year-round, with average temperatures ranging from 15 to 25 degrees Celsius (59 to 77 degrees Fahrenheit).

  • Vegetation

    Dry forests are home to a variety of drought-tolerant trees and shrubs. Some of the most common trees found in dry forests include acacia, baobab, mesquite, and oak. Shrubs such as creosote bush, sagebrush, and chaparral are also common. The understory of dry forests is often sparse, with grasses, wildflowers, and cacti.

  • Fauna

    Dry forests are home to a variety of animals, including mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, and insects. Some of the most common mammals found in dry forests include deer, antelope, elephants, and lions. Birds such as hawks, eagles, and vultures are also common. Reptiles such as snakes and lizards are also found in dry forests. Amphibians such as frogs and salamanders are less common in dry forests, but they can be found in areas with more permanent water sources. Insects such as ants, termites, and beetles are also common in dry forests.

  • Biodiversity

    Dry forests are not as biodiverse as tropical rainforests, but they are still home to a variety of plant and animal life. Dry forests are particularly important for wildlife because they provide habitat for many animals that are adapted to living in dry conditions.

  • Importance

    Dry forests play an important role in the global environment. They help to regulate the Earth's climate, prevent erosion, and provide clean water and air. Dry forests are also important cultural and spiritual sites for many indigenous peoples.

  • Threats

    Dry forests are facing a number of threats, including deforestation, climate change, and overgrazing. Deforestation is the clearing of forests for other uses, such as agriculture and development. This is a major problem because it destroys habitat and reduces biodiversity. Climate change is causing dry forests to become even drier, which is making them more vulnerable to wildfire and other disturbances. Overgrazing by livestock can also damage dry forests and prevent them from regenerating.

  • Conservation

    There are a number of efforts underway to conserve dry forests. These efforts include protected areas, sustainable forestry practices, and education campaigns. It is important to protect dry forests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

      Examples of subtropical forests around the world:

    • The Chaco dry forest in Argentina, Bolivia, and Paraguay
    • The Deccan thorn scrub forests in India
    • The Mediterranean dry forests in North Africa and southern Europe
    • The Miombo woodlands in Africa
    • The Sonoran Desert in the United States and Mexico

    • These dry forests are all home to a variety of unique plant and animal life, and they play an important role in the global environment.

    Monsoon forests are facing a number of threats, including deforestation, climate change, and pollution. Deforestation is the clearing of forests for other uses, such as agriculture and development. This is a major problem because it destroys habitat and reduces biodiversity. Climate change is causing monsoon forests to become warmer and drier, which is leading to changes in vegetation and wildlife. Pollution from factories and vehicles can also damage monsoon forests.

  • Conservation

    There are a number of efforts underway to conserve monsoon forests. These efforts include protected areas, sustainable forestry practices, and education campaigns. It is important to protect monsoon forests because they are essential to the health of the planet and the well-being of all people.

      Examples of monsoon forests around the world:

    • The Amazon rainforest in South America
    • The Congo rainforest in Africa
    • The Indo-Malayan rainforest in Southeast Asia
    • The Sundaland rainforest in Southeast Asia
    • The Western Ghats rainforest in India
    • The Sri Lankan rainforest in Sri Lanka

    • These monsoon forests are all home to a variety of unique plant and animal life, and they play an important role in the global environment.
